## Campaign Overview

The game will focus on:

- Large-scale conflict and strategy
- The pressure of a looming world threat
- Good ol' fashioned dungeon delving and exploration
- Political and faction-based choices
- Moments of villainy, resistance, and dramatic escalation

The overall structure is expected to have:

- A tense opening with the threat building early
- Significant downtime between major story beats
- Periodic dungeon crawling and action-focused adventure
- Restrictive resource management sections
- Plenty of mysteries to unravel...
- A climactic, Fishy-manufactured finale

## Game Format

- **Ruleset:** PHB 2024
- **Main Classes:** PHB 2024
- **Subclasses:** PHB 2024 + Forgotten Realms: Heroes of Faerûn
- **Spells:** PHB 2024 + Forgotten Realms: Heroes of Faerûn

## Character Creation

### Starting Characters

Players will begin the campaign by selecting from a set of **precreated origin characters** to play out the game's prologue. These are starting templates, not permanent locks. Each one is defined by:

- Background/backstory
- Class/subclass
- Narrative hooks
- A story role that fits the opening of the campaign

These characters are meant to make the prologue easier to start and give everyone a strong entry point into the story.

### Customization After the Prologue

Once the prologue ends, the origin characters can still be customized. This includes both story and presentation changes, subject to DM approval and campaign fit.

Possible changes include:

- Gender
- Presentation and appearance
- Personality details
- Background elements
- Personal goals
- Other character-facing details that do not break the current canon

### Replacement or Swapping

After the prologue concludes, a player may also:

- Swap origin characters with another player
- Replace their origin character entirely
- Rework the character into something closer to their preferred concept

The goal is to make sure everyone ends up with a character they enjoy playing.

### Death and Replacement Policy

If an origin character dies, no new origin characters will be created to replace them. In other words, any replacement character after that point will be made **custom**, together with the DM. This new character will be built to fit the campaign, the party, and the story situation at the time.

## World Overview

### The Cataclysm

The most defining event in this world’s history is **the Cataclysm**, which occurred **351 years before the start of the campaign**.

Legend says:

- The Kingpriests of Istar attempted to enforce divine order upon the world
- They were judged by the gods
- The gods responded by destroying and reshaping the world

Consequences included:

- Continental devastation
- Massive tsunamis and natural disasters
- Civilizations collapsing overnight

The setting is therefore **post-apocalyptic in nature**, even centuries later.

### The Silence of the Gods

Since the Cataclysm:

- The gods no longer manifest power directly
- No traditional clerics or paladins exist in normal circumstances
- Divine magic is effectively absent

However:

- The gods are still remembered
- Religions persist as belief systems and cultural memory
- There is no tangible proof of divine absence or presence in everyday life

### The Pantheon (Cultural Memory)

The gods are remembered but no longer demonstrably active. They are grouped into three alignments, with brief identities:

**Gods of Good**

- Paladine — justice, order, guardianship; patron of righteous leadership and dragons of good
- Branchala — music, joy, and life’s beauty
- Habbakuk — nature, animals, and natural balance
- Kiri-Jolith — honorable warfare and disciplined combat
- Majere — discipline, meditation, and restraint
- Mishakal — healing, compassion, and restoration
- Solinari — moon of white magic; patron of benevolent arcane arts

**Gods of Neutrality**

- Gilean — knowledge, records, and observation
- Chislev — nature in its untamed, instinctive form
- Lunitari — moon of neutral magic; illusion and balance in arcane arts
- Reorx — creation, craft, invention, and forging
- Shinare — trade, wealth, and industry
- Sirrion — fire, transformation, and creative chaos
- Zivilyn — wisdom across time and perspective

**Gods of Evil**

- Takhisis — the Dragon Queen; domination, conquest, and evil dragons
- Chemosh — undeath and false immortality
- Hiddukel — greed, betrayal, and corruption
- Morgion — disease, decay, and secrecy
- Nuitari — moon of black magic; selfish and destructive arcane power
- Sargonnas — vengeance, fire, and wrath
- Zeboim — storms, seas, and destructive chaos

These gods persist primarily as myth, theology, and cultural memory rather than active divine forces.

### Magic in the Setting

This is effectively a **low-magic campaign**:

- Divine magic is generally unavailable
- Arcane magic exists but is rare and politically controlled
- Most arcane users are tied to the **Mages of High Sorcery**

## Table Tone and Expectations

### Player Buy-In

Players should create characters who, in some way, want to oppose the Dragon Queen and survive the war around them.

Characters do not need to be traditionally good.

- Evil-aligned or morally questionable characters are allowed
- However, it should make sense that they are, at least initially, opposed to the Dragon Queen, and should ideally have a reason to undermine her invasion of Kalaman

This motivation can change over the course of the campaign.

Intra-party conflict is allowed, provided that:

- It leads to interesting character development
- It does not derail the game or prevent others from playing. Keep in mind, it is very hard to "derail" a campaign of mine due to my improv-maxxing, so need to be afraid to apply slightly edgy ideas.


## House Rules and Systems

### Encumbrance

This campaign will use **variant encumbrance rules**, which are already enabled on your D&D Beyond character sheets. This is an experimental change intended to make planning excursions and long-distance travel require some forethought. The goal is not to add arbitrary restrictions, but to make resource management a meaningful part of gameplay, and the campaign will be balanced around it accordingly.

If this system proves more frustrating than beneficial, I may scale it back to the standard encumbrance rules, which are more forgiving.

Another, more immersive inspiration for using encumbrance at all is meant to reflect that the characters are, physically speaking, closer to **ordinary people** operating under extreme circumstances.

If it at the end of the day, even regular encumbrance rules end up feeling too intrusive, we can remove encumbrance rules entirely, as we have in previous campaigns. I will be asking feedback on this regularly.

### Social Standing

The campaign will also use a **faction/renown system**.

This system tracks how different organizations perceive the party and how they respond to them over time.

Faction standing may influence:

- Access to support, equipment, or other resources
- Information, leads, and introductions
- Cooperation or resistance from key groups
- Expectations around how the party conducts itself
- Which approaches to objectives are encouraged or opposed

Your actions will shape these relationships. Some factions may grow more favorable toward you, while others may become distrustful or hostile. Over time, the party will develop a kind of social profile: certain groups may offer increasing benefits aligned with their interests, while others impose complications or penalties.

Renown is typically gained by working directly with a faction, but it can also shift through indirect actions, story events, or broader consequences of your decisions. Because the campaign includes meaningful downtime, you can also use that time to build relationships with specific factions or influential individuals.

### Large-Scale Battles (Experimental)

This campaign will include **large-scale battles**, and we will be experimenting with how to run them.

Primary tool:

- _Warriors of Krynn_ (board game integration)

Possible approaches (to be tested):

- Use the board game regularly for major battles
- Replace some battles with smaller, focused skirmishes
- Make large battles optional, where players can choose between:
    - Participating in the large-scale battle
    - Taking on smaller skirmishes within the fray with different rewards or consequences

We will adjust based on what feels most fun!

### Character Death and Tone

This is a **war campaign**, character and NPC death is expected to be part of the experience.

- Characters are not assumed to be untouchable heroes
- The mechanics (including encumbrance) reinforce a more grounded, physical reality
- Magic and divine elements exist, but do not remove the danger of war

Because of this, character deaths will often be treated as **blazes of glory** rather than abrupt or anticlimactic ends.

To guard against unsatisfying or incidental deaths for characters you have built up over time, I strongly favor giving players some degree of narrative and mechanical control in their final moments. At the player’s discretion, a character facing death may be given an opportunity to go out in a meaningful, impactful way.

This can include:

- A final surge of effort, momentary resilience, or mechanical edge before death
- The chance to protect allies, secure an objective, or change the outcome of a critical scene
- A decisive action that leaves a lasting mark on the story, the world, or the factions involved

The intent is not to remove danger or consequences, but to ensure that when a character falls, it feels earned and memorable. Death should reflect the arc of the character and the stakes of the moment, not a random or trivial misstep.

These moments will be handled collaboratively, with the player guiding how their character’s final stand unfolds within the situation at hand. The goal is for fallen characters to be remembered as **war heroes with defining last stands**, rather than as footnotes to chance (whenever given the chance).

### One-Turn Resolution (Experimental)

To avoid unnecessary or "padding" combat, we will experiment with a system I have coined a **one-turn**.

When the outcome of a fight is already clear (i.e., the party is going to win), we will not always play out the combat round by round.

Instead:

- Each player describes what they do in a single turn
- The encounter is then resolved cinematically from that point

This creates a brief “bullet-time” moment that allows for:

- Creative, vivid descriptions
- Coordinated or combo-style actions
- Faster pacing without losing narrative payoff

This approach does **not** mean that certain combats have no consequences any more. Rather, the costs and benefits will be shaped by _how_ you describe resolving the situation.

For example, simply saying “I swing at all the goblins in front of me” will work—but it will likely result in you taking significant damage, which will be applied accordingly. On the other hand, creative, tactical, or stylish approaches can reduce risk, improve outcomes, or create additional advantages.

You are encouraged to:

- Propose clever or cinematic solutions
- Coordinate with teammates for combo-style actions
- Engage with the environment or enemy weaknesses

For instance, a fighter launching an ally into the air so they can rain attacks from above, bypassing enemy shields, is the kind of coordinated and inventive play that will be rewarded with a cleaner, more effective resolution.

If an idea pushes the boundaries of plausibility, I may call for a roll to determine how well it works. In general, however, the more creative and engaging the approach, the more I will try to support it and make it succeed in a satisfying way.

The goal is not adversarial play. In these sort of moments, I am not trying to “beat” you; we are collaborating to create memorable, cinematic moments and the best story possible together.

### Player Impact in the War

Although you are individuals in a massive conflict, your actions will matter significantly.

- Successes will save lives, secure advantages, and shape outcomes
- Failures will cost lives and weaken your side
- Most individual failures will not instantly lose the war
- However, repeated failures will accumulate

If enough goes wrong over time, **the war can be lost, and with it the campaign**. Do not forget this.

### Themes

Beyond the central good-versus-evil conflict, the campaign may explore:

- Famine
- Economic strain and collapse
- Discrimination and social tension
- The realities and costs of wartime
- Moral ambiguity in decision-making

Despite these elements, the overarching structure remains largely **good versus evil**, with room for complexity in how that struggle plays out.
