> Darkness. Peaceful darkness. There was no sound, no space to move—only a quiet, suffocating stillness that felt almost like mercy. He thought he could sleep here, drift away without consequence. Mother would come wake him soon, he told himself. She always did. There was nothing to fear.
> 
> No. Mother was dead. She had been for years. So was the boy—the one who had trusted him, believed in him. The one he had failed. The thought passed through him with a dull, familiar ache. Such was the way of life. What was one man against the madness of this world? He had learned long ago that he could not change what was coming. The best anyone could do was let go.
> 
> Yes, I think I will stay here, a little while longer...
> 
> Then he gasped awake.
> 
> Air tore into his lungs, thick with ash, and he coughed violently, expelling soot and dust. His ears rang with a high, relentless whine. Something heavy pinned his ribs, grinding pain into every breath, but he forced himself to move. Stone shifted. His body protested. Still, he pushed upward.
> 
> I’m stronger now, he told himself. I can protect them this time. But only if I get up. Now.
> 
> He staggered to his feet and immediately doubled over, retching. The taste of salted pork burned his throat as he emptied his stomach onto the scorched ground. His eyes stung fiercely, tears streaming as smoke filled the air. He blinked through it, vision swimming, trying to make sense of the world around him.
> 
> The ringing in his ears began to fade. In its place came the screams.
> 
> Men. Women. Children.
> 
> The sounds cut through him more sharply than any wound. How had it come to this? Why was he here again? His thoughts struggled to align, fractured by pain and shock. Then it returned to him—the explosion. Of course. He scanned the destruction, searching for any sign of his company. Anyone. But deep down, he already knew.
> 
> None of them had made it.
> 
> He was cursed. He had always believed that.
> 
> Still, he would fight. Until his last breath, he would fight.

> As you feverishly stumble along the soot-stricken streets, through the drifting smoke, shapes emerge—twisted, grotesque figures rushing toward you with unnatural speed. You have no strength left to give, no plan, no hope. Their eyes burn with a cold, knowing hatred, and where the firelight catches them, their forms shimmer with a scaled, ember-lit sheen, as though the flames themselves cling to their skin. They are destruction incarnate. You brandish your sword, bracing for impact.
> 
> A cry—weak, desperate.
> 
> Off to your left, beneath a half-collapsed beam, someone is trapped. A young soldier, barely more than a boy, bloodied but alive. His hand reaches out toward you, trembling.
> 
> "Please…"
> 
> The creatures are closing in.
> 
> What do you do?
> 
> 1. Do you rush to him, trying to lift the beam before the abominations reach you both?
> 2. Do you drag him free as quickly as you can, risking further injury to get him away?
> 3. Do you stand between him and the oncoming horrors, buying him time to escape?
> 
> There is no time to think. Only to act.
> 
> 1. With a desperate heave, it shifts—enough. He slips free, collapsing into your arms, breathless and alive. Relief flickers for a heartbeat. A blur of scales and fire crashes down from the broken roof, and before you can react, it drives into him—fast, precise. His body jerks in your grasp, eyes wide with shock. The life leaves him as suddenly as it returned.
> 2. You drag him free, heedless of his cries, pulling him from the wreckage and forcing both of you forward. You stagger together through smoke and ruin, his weight dragging at you, your strength failing with every step. The creatures close the distance faster than you can flee. You feel the impact, hear his voice break into a scream—cut short. Blades, claws, something sharp and merciless strikes him again and again as you’re dragged down beside him.
> 3. Steel meets claw, and for a moment, you hold them—driven by fury, by defiance, by the need to give him time. Behind you, you hear him struggling, trying to crawl free. A crack above—too late. Rubble gives way, stone crashing down. You don’t even have time to turn. The sound it makes is final, sickening. When the dust settles, there is no movement behind you.
> 
> Enraged, you cut down the remaining assailants. Once the threats are neutralized, the strength drains from you all at once. Your legs buckle, and you fall hard to your knees, horror flooding through you. You stare at where another life, looking to you to be their shield, had once been. Not this. You failed. Again.
> 
> You lift your gaze to the smoke-stained sky, searching for an answer from gods who no longer answer.
> 
> Is this why they abandoned us?
> 
> How can men stand against such reckless, unrelenting hatred? Is this their great cleansing? Was the cataclysm just the beginning?
> 
> For a brief moment, the stars still shine—cold and distant, untouched by the devastation below. Then a vast shadow passes over them, swallowing their light. Something enormous descends, both magnificent and terrible, its presence pressing down upon the world like a final judgment.
> 
> From a nearby street, a voice cries out—a single, terrible word.
> 
> "No… it can’t be."
> 
> They were gone. They were supposed to be gone.
> 
> All is lost.
> 
> And then the sky burns, and your world is consumed by fire and ash.
  
# Act 1: Premonition

## Summary

This act loosely follows the plot of the first half of [[04-chapter-3-when-home-burns|Chapter 3: When Home Burns]]. In this act, all the characters will slowly enter the story and be allocated to a player. The relatively quiet and positive narrative will culminate with the battle at High Hill, where the final character will be revealed. This storyline will mostly adopt the adaptions made in the companion guide.

> The year is 351 AC—three hundred and fifty-one years since the Cataclysm that, according to legend, shattered the world and reshaped the fate of Krynn. It is the 13th of Fleurgreen, that brief turning of the season when spring yields to the first promise of summer. Morning rests lightly over the land: a gentle breeze stirs the treetops, birds call from scattered brooks, and a thin mist clings to dew-soaked grass and undergrowth. The air is mild beneath a sky veiled with soft, drifting cloud. There is a quiet clarity to it all—so calm and ordinary that, without history to remind you otherwise, the world feels unbroken.
> 
> You ride westward through Hinterlund’s borderlands, escorting a pair of nobles, with Becklin riding the right flank as a fellow Knight of Solamnia—older, more experienced, a Knight of the Crown whose steady presence sets the pace of the group without formal command. The Vingaard River is now behind you, crossed by ferry earlier under the watch of drifting boats and quiet traffic, and the road ahead carries you west through riverlands into bordering forest; here the path narrows into a worn track between trees and undergrowth as Becklin leads with quiet certainty born of local familiarity, Vogler lying somewhere further along the route.
> 
> At the edge of the Solamnic province of Hinterlund lies your destination: the quiet fishing village of Vogler. It is a place spoken of as the end of the road, where little changes and less is expected. You had never been there yourself, but Ispin Greenshield spoke often of it in passing, as had Becklin on the rare occasions your paths crossed with her, both describing its people, routines, and quiet resilience with a familiarity that set it apart from any other stop along the road. To the north stretch scattered woods and brooks that slowly give way to the harsh and unforgiving Northern Wastes. To the south, beyond the river you crossed earlier, lies Nightlund—a land of darker reputation and uncertain promise—beyond which stands Kalaman, a city of trade and distant connections.
> 
> The nobles under escort were locals from Vogler, returning for the Kingfisher Festival. The Bakarises made the road feel longer than it was.
> 
> The elder complained at every turn—the mud, the weather, the pace, the road itself—as though the world had insulted him personally. His son, Bakaris the Younger, said less, but what he did say landed sharper, with the quiet certainty of superiority.
> 
> At the younger Bakaris’s side trudged a scaled, hunched creature. It walked where the others rode, heavy-limbed and exhausted, its form hard to place at a glance. Even near Kalaman, where law was supposed to mean something, serfdom still existed.
> 
> Bakaris the elder looked down at the lizard-man. “Useful. At least one of us knows how to keep up. Isn't that right, Murk?”
> 
> The creature did not respond.
> 
> Becklin kept her gaze forward. “I would advise slowing the pace. The north is not far, and it would be wise not to draw attention.”
> 
> Bakaris the Younger gave a thin smile. “Solamnic discipline now includes telling people when to be afraid?”
> 
> “It includes restraint,” Becklin said.
> 
> The elder Bakaris snorted. “Restraint didn’t stop the Cataclysm.”
> 
> “The Knights were not charged with stopping it alone,” Becklin replied. “Only with holding what could still be held.”
> 
> He waved a hand. “Convenient framing for failure.”
> 
> Becklin’s tone stayed even. “Survival is duty, not virtue.”
> 
> The elder scoffed. “Duty. Oaths. Crown, Sword, Rose. Sounds like accounting.”
> 
> “They are structures,” Becklin said. “Crown for loyalty. Sword for courage. Rose for wisdom.”
> 
> The Younger Bakaris tilted his head. “And you are?”
> 
> “Crown,” she said. “As is he.” A nod toward you. “Service first. Not certainty.”
> 
> Bakaris the elder gave a short laugh. “Service. That’s what you call it?”
> 
> “Even to the worst of us. Rules matter when things go wrong.”
> 
> “They didn’t stop the Cataclysm.”
> 
> “No,” Becklin said. “But they tell you what is worth saving.”
> 
> Bakaris’s mouth curled. “Speaking of worth saving—Lord Soth.”
> 
> Becklin’s expression tightened.
> 
> “Which order was he again? Crown, Sword, Rose?” he asked. “Hard to keep track once a knight goes bad.”
> 
> “You know the tale,” Becklin said.
> 
> “I know the punchline.” He glanced at you, amused. “Maybe they should have made him a squire first. Saved everyone the trouble.”
> 
> Bakaris the Younger gave a faint snort.
> 
> Becklin held his gaze. “You know the story. Not the truth.”
> 
> “Then the truth is simple,” Bakaris said. “Oaths don’t matter if nobody enforces them.”
