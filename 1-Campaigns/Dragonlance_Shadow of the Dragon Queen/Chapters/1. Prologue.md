# Act 0

## Summary

You made it! This is to be read at the start of the campaign, to serve as ominous foreshadowing during the events of the Prelude. This completely replaces the "Prelude to War" chapter in the official module, with the character's connections to Ispin Greenshield and sorcery trials unfolding off-screen in the players' backstories.

## Introduction

**Music:** [Battle In City](ttrpg-sound:Soundbar/Ambience/Battle In City.mp3)

> [!readaloud] 
> Darkness. Peaceful darkness. There was no sound, no space to move—only a quiet, suffocating stillness that felt almost like mercy. He thought he could sleep here, drift away without consequence. Mother would come wake him soon, he told himself. She always did. There was nothing to fear.
> 
> Then the memory came, sudden and merciless. Mother was dead. She had been for years. So was the boy—the one who had trusted him, believed in him. The one he had failed. The thought passed through him with a dull, familiar ache. Such was the way of life. What was one man against the madness of this world? He had learned long ago that he could not change what was coming. The best anyone could do was let go.
> 
> Yes, I think I will stay here, a little while longer...
> 
> Then he gasped awake.
> 
> Air tore into his lungs, thick with ash, and he coughed violently, expelling soot and dust. His ears rang with a high, relentless whine. Something heavy pinned his ribs, grinding pain into every breath, but he forced himself to move. Stone shifted. His body protested. Still, he pushed upward.
> 
> I’m stronger now, he told himself. I can protect them this time. But only if I get up. Now.
> 
> He staggered to his feet and immediately doubled over, retching. The taste of salted pork burned his throat as he emptied his stomach onto the scorched ground. His eyes stung fiercely, tears streaming as smoke filled the air. He blinked through it, vision swimming, trying to make sense of the world around him.
> 
> The ringing in his ears began to fade. In its place came the screams.
> 
> Men. Women. Children.
> 
> The sounds cut through him more sharply than any wound. How had it come to this? Why was he here again? His thoughts struggled to align, fractured by pain and shock. Then it returned to him—the explosion. Of course. He scanned the destruction, searching for any sign of his company. Anyone. But deep down, he already knew.
> 
> None of them had made it.
> 
> He was cursed. He had always believed that.
> 
> Still, he would fight. Until his last breath, he would fight.

> [!readaloud] 
> Through the drifting smoke, shapes emerge—twisted, grotesque figures rushing toward you with unnatural speed. You have no strength left to give, no plan, no hope. Their eyes burn with a cold, knowing hatred, and where the firelight catches them, their forms shimmer with a scaled, ember-lit sheen, as though the flames themselves cling to their skin. They are destruction incarnate.
> 
> A cry—weak, desperate.
> 
> Off to your left, beneath a half-collapsed beam, someone is trapped. A young soldier, barely more than a boy, bloodied but alive. His hand reaches out toward you, trembling.
> 
> "Please…"
> 
> The creatures are closing in.
> 
> What do you do?
> 
> 1. Do you rush to him, trying to lift the beam before the abominations reach you both?
> 2. Do you drag him free as quickly as you can, risking further injury to get him away?
> 3. Do you stand between him and the oncoming horrors, buying him time to escape?
> 
> There is no time to think. Only to act.
> 
> 1. With a desperate heave, it shifts—enough. He slips free, collapsing into your arms, breathless and alive. Relief flickers for a heartbeat. A blur of scales and fire crashes down from the broken roof, and before you can react, it drives into him—fast, precise. His body jerks in your grasp, eyes wide with shock. The life leaves him as suddenly as it returned.
> 2. You drag him free, heedless of his cries, pulling him from the wreckage and forcing both of you forward. You stagger together through smoke and ruin, his weight dragging at you, your strength failing with every step. The creatures close the distance faster than you can flee. You feel the impact, hear his voice break into a scream—cut short. Blades, claws, something sharp and merciless strikes him again and again as you’re dragged down beside him.
> 3. Steel meets claw, and for a moment, you hold them—driven by fury, by defiance, by the need to give him time. Behind you, you hear him struggling, trying to crawl free. A crack above—too late. Rubble gives way, stone crashing down. You don’t even have time to turn. The sound it makes is final, sickening. When the dust settles, there is no movement behind you.
> 
> The strength drains from you all at once. Your legs buckle, and you fall hard to your knees, horror flooding through you. Not this. You failed. Again.
> 
> You lift your gaze to the smoke-stained sky, searching for an answer from gods who no longer answer.
> 
> Is this why they abandoned us?
> 
> How can men stand against such reckless, unrelenting hatred?
> 
> For a brief moment, the stars still shine—cold and distant, untouched by the devastation below. Then a vast shadow passes over them, swallowing their light. Something enormous descends, both magnificent and terrible, its presence pressing down upon the world like a final judgment.
> 
> From a nearby street, a voice cries out—a single, terrible word.
> 
> "No… it can’t be."
> 
> They were gone. They were supposed to be gone.
> 
> All is lost.
> 
> And then the sky burns, and your world is consumed by fire and ash.
  
# Act 1: Premonition

## Summary

This act loosely follows the plot of [[04-chapter-3-when-home-burns|Chapter 3: When Home Burns]]. In this act, all the characters will slowly enter the story and be allocated to a player. The relatively quiet and positive narrative will culminate with the battle at high hill, where the final character will be revealed.

## Introduction

**Music:** [1-05 - Prelude](ttrpg-sound:Soundbar/Music/1-05 - Prelude.mp3)
**Sources:**
- [[1-Preludes_Expanded.pdf]]

> [!readaloud] 
> The year is 351 AC—three hundred and fifty-one years since the Cataclysm that, according to legend, shattered the world and reshaped the fate of Krynn. It is the 13th of Fleurgreen, that brief turning of the season when spring yields to the first promise of summer. Morning rests lightly over the land: a gentle breeze stirs the treetops, birds call from scattered brooks, and a thin mist clings to dew-soaked grass and undergrowth. The air is mild beneath a sky veiled with soft, drifting cloud. There is a quiet clarity to it all—so calm and ordinary that, without history to remind you otherwise, the world feels unbroken.
> 
> You ride westward through Hinterlund’s borderlands, escorting a pair of nobles alongside Becklin and others who have taken the same vows as you. Becklin is a fellow Knight of Solamnia—older, more experienced, a Knight of the Crown whose presence naturally sets a steadier pace for the group, though she does not command it in any formal sense. The rhythm of hooves and the weight of armor are familiar, but the purpose behind this journey sits heavier than most.
> 
> After crossing the Vingaard River, you made landfall some distance west of Vogler, ferried across its slow, murky waters under the watch of drifting fishing boats and quiet river traffic. From there, your party turned eastward on horseback, now advancing through the bordering forest toward the village. The road here is little more than a worn passage between trees and undergrowth, but Becklin guides the way with quiet certainty, drawing on familiarity with the region as Vogler itself comes closer ahead.
> 
> At the edge of the Solamnic province of Hinterlund lies your destination: the quiet fishing village of Vogler. It is a place spoken of as the end of the road, where little changes and less is expected. You had never been there yourself, but Becklin—who hails from Vogler—told you of it before you set out. She described its people, its routines, and its quiet resilience with a familiarity that sets it apart from any other stop along the road. To the north stretch scattered woods and brooks that slowly give way to the harsh and unforgiving Northern Wastes. To the south, beyond the river you crossed earlier, lies Nightlund—a land of darker reputation and uncertain promise—beyond which stands Kalaman, a city of trade and distant connections.
> 
> As you approach, Vogler reveals itself slowly. The village clings to a narrow spit of land reaching into the river, its wooden homes clustered tightly together as if for comfort against the wider world. Cliffs rise behind it, crowned by the ivy-choked remains of a crumbling keep, watching silently over the settlement below. The only road descends from that height, guiding all who arrive directly into the heart of the village.
> 
> Dominating the river is a structure that does not belong to this age: an immense, unfinished stone bridge, its craftsmanship far surpassing anything built in recent memory. It stretches partway across the Vingaard before ending abruptly, replaced by simple ferries—ropes and rafts that carry travelers the rest of the way. Around it, small fishing boats drift across the slow, murky waters, their presence lending the village a quiet, persistent life.
> 
> Becklin notes, as the village comes into clearer view, that you have arrived at an opportune time. The Kingfisher Festival is underway—a local celebration meant to bring music, color, and warmth to an otherwise modest place. Even from a distance, there are signs of it: movement along the docks, decorations catching the light, the faint suggestion of gathering crowds.
> 
> Under normal circumstances, you might have welcomed it. A festival meant rest, distraction, perhaps even a moment of ease among your duties.
> 
> But not this time.
> 
> You know what awaits in Vogler. You volunteered for this assignment because of it. And as the village draws nearer, the weight of that choice settles in your chest, heavier than steel, dulling whatever comfort the festival might have offered.
> 
> But you, Edric of Brackenridge, had to see it through. For an old friend.

> [!readaloud] 
> You rode with Becklin and the others toward Vogler under a steady, unsettled sky, the road long enough for tempers to show themselves and long enough for Becklin to keep them in check. She remained in the saddle with the calm of a veteran knight, speaking only when necessary, her voice level and precise. Beside her rode Gareth, her squire, attentive and earnest. He watched her with clear admiration, trying hard to match her discipline and failing only in the quiet confidence that comes with age.
> 
> The nobles under escort were locals from Vogler itself, returning home for the Kingfisher Festival. Lord Bakaris made the journey feel longer than it was. He complained about the road, the weather, the pace, and nearly everything else, each remark sharpened by the certainty that the world should bend more readily to his convenience.
> 
> His son—Bakaris the Younger—proved little improvement. Where his father was openly abrasive, the younger man was sharper in a quieter, more insidious way: every observation carried the tone of a man convinced of his own superiority, every pause measured as though the company itself was beneath him. When the elder Bakaris grumbled about the pace, his son corrected him under his breath with thinly veiled disdain. When the Knights of Solamnia were mentioned, he smiled as though recalling an inside joke no one else was clever enough to understand. The two of them seemed less like companions and more like competing egos bound by blood and mutual contempt.
> 
> Walking at Bakaris’s side was a scaled, hunched figure—some manner of humanoid monstrosity whose exact nature was unclear at a glance. It moved upright, but awkwardly, its form marked by heavy scales and an unnatural silhouette that set it apart from anything common in Solamnia. It walked rather than rode, trudging along the roadside while the rest of the party remained mounted.
> 
> Despite what it was, you found yourself feeling a measure of pity for it. The road was muddy in places, and every step it took seemed heavier than the last, as if it had long since stopped expecting ease from the world. Even here, so close to Kalaman—the so-called beacon of the east, where law and order were said to hold firmer than elsewhere—petty slavery was still legal, and the creature’s presence was a quiet reminder of how unevenly that order was applied.
> 
> Bakaris, of course, still found ways to be unpleasant about him.
> 
> "Useful," Bakaris said at one point, glancing toward the scaled figure as though inspecting a pack animal. "At least one of us knows how to keep up."
> 
> Becklin did not rise to the bait. "He keeps up better than your manners," she replied, not turning in the saddle.
> 
> Bakaris the Younger gave a thin, amused smile. "How noble. I didn’t realize Solamnic training included mastering the art of defending property you didn’t choose to own."
> 
> Becklin’s gaze finally shifted toward him, calm but firm. "Solamnic training includes restraint. Something your family appears to interpret as optional."
> 
> The elder Bakaris scoffed. "Restraint didn’t save the world, did it? Knights, gods, all of it—Cataclysm still came."
> 
> That earned a brief silence from Becklin, though not one of uncertainty. "The Knights of Solamnia are not charged with saving the world alone," she said evenly. "They are charged with standing between what is broken and what remains worth protecting."
> 
> Bakaris the Younger tilted his head. "And what, precisely, is that supposed to mean?"
> 
> "It means," Becklin replied, "that failure does not erase purpose."
> 
> Gareth, riding nearby, shifted slightly in his saddle as the conversation turned heavier. Your squire, Tomas Vale, listened with the careful attention of someone trying to decide which version of adulthood was real.
> 
> Bakaris the Younger laughed softly. "Purpose is just what people say when they don’t want to admit they lost."
> 
> "Then you misunderstand us," Becklin said. "The Knights of the Crown swear loyalty and obedience. The Knights of the Sword swear honor, courage, and reverence. And the Knights of the Rose—those who earn the highest station—are expected to temper all of it with wisdom and justice."
> 
> She continued without pause, voice steady. "We are not one thing. We are three orders, bound together by discipline, not pride."
> 
> Bakaris the Younger smirked. "And which are you, then?"
> 
> "A Knight of the Crown," Becklin answered. "As is he." A nod toward you. "We begin with service. Not certainty."
> 
> The younger Bakaris leaned back slightly in his saddle. "Service," he echoed, as if tasting something mildly unpleasant. "How inspiring."
> 
> Bakaris the elder gave a dismissive wave. "All of it sounds like elaborate apologizing for incompetence."
> 
> Becklin did not respond immediately. When she did, her tone was quieter. "There is a story among knights. Lord Soth of Knightlund."
> 
> That name shifted the air slightly. Even Bakaris the Younger seemed to pay attention, though only in the way one listens for weakness.
> 
> "A knight once counted among the greatest of his age," Becklin continued. "Respected, feared, even admired. But in time, pride and suspicion consumed him. He was cast out, and his name became a warning more than a memory."
> 
> Bakaris the Younger interrupted. "Oh, I know that story. The ‘fallen knight.’ The one who killed his own wife and doomed everything."
> 
> Becklin’s expression did not change. "You know _a_ story. Not necessarily the truth of it."
> 
> Bakaris the elder leaned forward slightly. "The truth is simple enough. He failed. Like all of them failed when the Cataclysm came."
> 
> "He was not tasked with preventing the Cataclysm alone," Becklin said. "And much of what is said of him is shaped by fear, not fact."
> 
> Bakaris the Younger waved a hand dismissively. "So what, he’s misunderstood? A tragic hero? How convenient."
> 
> Becklin’s voice remained measured. "What is convenient is reducing every knight, every oath, every failure, to a single story that confirms what you already believe."
> 
> For a moment, even Bakaris did not immediately answer.
> 
> Gareth glanced toward the other squire, and the shared silence between them felt heavier than the earlier arguments.
> 
> When Bakaris finally spoke again, it was with a shrug. "Fine. Believe what you like. I still say the world doesn’t care about oaths."
> 
> "It does not have to," Becklin replied. "Oaths are for those who choose to care anyway."
